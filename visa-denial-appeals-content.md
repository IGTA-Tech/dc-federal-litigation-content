## When USCIS Denies Your Petition

USCIS denies thousands of visa petitions each year. Many denials are based on errors, misinterpretation of evidence, or improper application of legal standards.

Administrative appeals through AAO can take years and often rubber-stamp initial denials. But you have another option: challenging the denial in federal court.

DC Federal Litigation PLLC litigates visa denial challenges in federal district court, seeking to overturn arbitrary and capricious USCIS decisions.

---

## When Can You Challenge a Visa Denial in Federal Court?

### Exhaustion Requirements

Generally, you must exhaust administrative remedies before filing in federal court. This means:
- Filing an administrative appeal (AAO or appropriate forum)
- Receiving a final agency decision
- Or demonstrating that exhaustion would be futile

### Exceptions to Exhaustion

Courts may excuse exhaustion when:
- Administrative appeal would be futile
- Agency position is clearly established
- Irreparable harm would result from delay
- Legal issue requires no additional factual development

### Timing

The statute of limitations for APA challenges is typically six years, but you should act promptly after final denial.

### L-1 Petition Denials

L-1 intracompany transferee petitions face scrutiny on:

- Managerial or executive capacity (L-1A)
- Specialized knowledge (L-1B)
- One year employment abroad

**We challenge denials based on:**
- Improper specialized knowledge standards
- Arbitrary managerial capacity findings
- Misapplication of qualifying relationship rules

### EB-5 Investor Petition Denials

EB-5 denials involve complex issues:
- Source of funds documentation

**We challenge denials based on:**

## Naturalization Denial Lawsuits

### Special Rules for Naturalization

Naturalization applications have unique federal court review rights under 8 U.S.C. § 1447(b):

**120-Day Rule:**
If USCIS fails to make a determination within 120 days of your examination, you can file in federal district court.

**De Novo Review:**
The court conducts de novo (fresh) review, not just review of the administrative record.

**Court Options:**
The court may:
- Determine the naturalization application itself
- Remand to USCIS with instructions to decide within 30 days

### Common Naturalization Denial Issues

- Good moral character challenges
- Continuous residence questions
- Physical presence calculations
- English and civics test issues
- Attachment to Constitution

## The Legal Standard: Arbitrary and Capricious

### APA Review

Federal courts review USCIS decisions under the Administrative Procedure Act (5 U.S.C. § 706). The primary standard is whether the agency action was "arbitrary, capricious, an abuse of discretion, or otherwise not in accordance with law."

### What Makes a Decision Arbitrary and Capricious?

- Failed to consider relevant factors
- Relied on improper factors
- Made clear error of judgment
- Failed to articulate rational connection between facts and decision
- Applied incorrect legal standard
- Failed to consider evidence

### Chevron Deference

Courts give deference to agency interpretations of ambiguous statutes, but this deference has limits. USCIS interpretations that are unreasonable or contrary to statutory purpose are not entitled to deference.

## The Litigation Process

### Filing the Complaint

Your complaint will:
- Identify the denial being challenged
- Allege violations of the APA
- Request declaratory and injunctive relief
- Seek remand with instructions

### Administrative Record

The case is typically decided on the administrative record—the documents USCIS considered in making its decision. We analyze the record for:
- Favorable evidence USCIS ignored
- Improper standards applied
- Errors in reasoning

### Briefing

Parties file cross-motions for summary judgment or motions for judgment on the record. Briefs argue:
- Whether USCIS applied correct legal standards
- Whether decision was supported by the record
- Whether USCIS considered all relevant evidence

### Oral Argument

Courts may hold oral argument on the motions.

### Decision

If you prevail, the court typically remands to USCIS with instructions. The court may order:
- Approval of the petition
- Reconsideration under correct standards
- Additional fact-finding

## Benefits of Federal Court Challenge

### Faster Resolution

While AAO appeals can take years, federal court litigation often resolves in months.

### Neutral Decision-Maker

Federal judges are independent Article III judges, not USCIS employees.

### Different Standards

Courts apply rigorous APA review, not the deferential approach AAO often applies.

### Binding Precedent

Court decisions create binding precedent that affects future cases.

### Settlement Leverage

Litigation creates pressure for favorable settlement.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I have to appeal to AAO first?**
Generally yes, unless exhaustion would be futile. We evaluate each case to determine whether exhaustion is required or can be excused.

**How long does federal court litigation take?**
Cases typically resolve within 6-12 months, though complex cases may take longer. This compares favorably to AAO appeals that often take 2+ years.

**What are my chances of winning?**
Success depends on the strength of your case and the nature of USCIS's errors. We evaluate likelihood of success during consultation.

**Can I get approved in federal court?**
Courts can order USCIS to approve a petition in appropriate cases, though more commonly they remand with instructions. Upon remand, USCIS often approves rather than repeat the same errors.

**What does this cost?**
We discuss fees during consultation. Costs depend on case complexity. Attorney's fees may be recoverable if you substantially prevail.

**Can I stay in the US while the case is pending?**
Immigration status during litigation depends on your current status and circumstances. We can discuss this during consultation.
